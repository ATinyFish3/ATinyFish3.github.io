# Elephant Hunting

Whilst reading through [the EA Handbook](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/handbook) I reached the [Radical Empathy](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/s/QMrYGgBvg64JhcQrS/p/ehZK259et52Xnvw5F) section and specifically the idea of the 'expanding circle' and had some ideas that I think might be worth writing down.

## The Expanding Circle

From the EA Handbook:

`In The Expanding Circle, Peter Singer discusses how, over the course of history, "The circle of altruism has broadened from the family and tribe to the nation and race ... to all human beings" (and adds that "the process should not stop there").`

The examples given include Plato, with the aim of improving morality, arguing that Greeks should only enslave, lay waste to lands and raze houses of non-Greeks, Romans viewing barbarians as fair game for capturing, enslaving and being forced to fight in the Colosseum for entertainment. Europeans figuring out that these acts are bad... unless the people you wanted to enslave were non-European. It seems clear that in the past the boundaries of who deserves moral consideration have been worryingly small and each improvement still lacking completeness. 

But we've figured all that out now right? Our modern moral circle is surely as wide as can be?

## Our Modern Moral Circle is Not as Wide as Can Be

Take Myanmar - where the Muslim Rohingya people are [currently being persecuted and killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohingya_genocide), or China - where the Uyghurs are experiencing [ongoing human rights abuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_genocide). Groups being treated appallingly due to them being outside the moral circle of those in power. 

But *we* know this is wrong - it's obvious.

Or we can look at killing dogs. In the West dogs are in our moral circle - abusing a dog is just so obviously wrong and evil; the thought of killing and eating them, as is done elsewhere, is unthinkable. We know almost innately that killing a dog is bad.

But now I'm going to take a leaf out of [Rationality: From AI to Zombies](https://www.lesswrong.com/tag/rationality:-from-ai-to-zombies) - why is killing a dog wrong? This is not to say it isn't but to point out that maybe this thought is our brains 'completing the pattern'. Your parents told you that hitting a dog is bad, you've never been served a dog at a restaurant so there's no pattern in your head that links from dog to food. 

But have you *thought* about why its bad - actually sat down with the hypothesis that "killing dogs is bad"? What critiques of this could you think of? What arguments would you have against those critiques? "Maybe dog meat tastes really good" you could critique and "the pleasure of any taste cannot outweigh a life that has moral value" you might respond. 

You can do this with previous examples too - Plato isn't wrong just because obviously he's wrong. If you travelled back in time to convince Plato that he should expand his moral circle to include more than just Greeks what critiques of your hypothesis would he make, what would you say in response to them? Perhaps Plato says that non-Greeks are less intelligent  so should not be given moral consideration but you know that this is false and that, even if it were true you know it would be wrong to treat people badly if they did poorly in a test.

But then you have to imagine a time traveller from the future, or your children, or grandchildren. They come back to tell us that we need to expand our moral circle - who would they think we need to include but aren't? To them there is currently an elephant in the room - we currently cannot see or comprehend expanding our moral circle but in the future, just as we look back on slavery, it will be blindingly obvious the mistakes of today.

Answering this question is difficult; it requires looking it ordinary, normal beliefs, thinking about these beliefs and coming to the conclusion that this normal thing, that you and everyone you know is actually obviously bad. It seems likely to me that non-human animals should be in our moral circle - and if this seems unlikely to you then try thinking of all the points you would make to someone in China that thinks eating dog is okay that it isn't and imagine someone making those same points to you about other animals. I also do not think that the issues a time traveller or future generations can see we currently have will be limited to issues of our moral circle - the elephants in the room could be in many areas of our current beliefs.

## Elephant Hunting

In [Rationality: From AI to Zombies](https://www.lesswrong.com/tag/rationality:-from-ai-to-zombies) Eliezer Yudkowsky proposes that we should sit and think for 5 miutes, by the clock, and try and critique our own beliefs - actually properly  try and convince yourself that you are wrong; because you just might be. Doing this for every belief that you and everyone you know currently holds is probably an inefficient way of trying to find these 'elephants in the room' but based on our long history of being wrong it seems likely that we are and will continue to be wrong about many things. Maybe when artificial general intelligence comes along we should include it on our moral circle, this seems odd to me - its just some code right?, but a response like that almost doesn't make sense; it's just people from another country, right? It's just some people from another continent, right? It's just a dog, right? It's just an animal, right?

It seems likely there are currently more 'elephants in the room' than we would like to admit - but that's kind of the point. Remaining vigilant for them and questioning your beliefs (and actually being willing to change your beliefs if your questioning breaks them down) - hunting these elephants - should be constant and brutal.

The herd is large. The herd is much smaller. The herd can be smaller still.

Happy Hunting!
