# Correcter Horse Battery Staple

*My, probably unfounded, take on good passwords*

## Lengthhhhhh

Passwords should be long above all else. 

Replacing an 'o' with a '0' is already part of a ruleset for trying to crack passwords, adding '123' to the end, ending a '!' at the end, etc... add very little to the security of your password. Length is king.

## Correct Horse Battery Staple

The popular [xkcd comic](https://xkcd.com/936/) about password strength suggests picking (or hopefully generating) four random words, shoving them together and using that as a password. This is good at making longer passwords that are easier to remember than some randomly generated characters however there is room for improvement.

In [this Computerphile video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3NjQ9b3pgIg), it is suggested to also include a more uncommon word and some numbers/special characters (though not dumped at the end of course). There is still room for improvement here - if you don't want to use a password manager as the video concludes.

## Correcter Horse Battery Staple

I believe that the main flaw with this method is that whilst four words may be easier to remember than a random string of characters; those four words only cover you for 1 password. How many accounts do you have that, hopefully, use different passwords. Very quickly four words becomes forty - and that's before you make ten of those uncommon words that are harder to remember off the bat.

My suggestion is thus: pick *one* word that is in someway, to you, related to the account you are using the password for; making a Google account pick 'search', Facebook: 'friend'... 

Take this word and add *something* to it an exclamation mark, your dog's birthday, whatever. 

So now we might have: 'searh20150814'

Now, for length and ease of memory, describe the password: 'search20150814dogsbirthdayontheend' or you could have 'searchdogsbirthdayontheend20150814'.

Now you have a very long password where knowing the password helps you remember the password and remembering the password *is* the password; you don't have to a story about a horse saying 'that's a battery staple', you just remember the story that tells you your password explicitly - 'the password is search with dog's birthday on the end'. Memorability increases with length; even better you could have 'search20150814!withdogsbirthdayontheendthenanupsidedowni' etc...

## Misc

As a sidenote; if the thing you're signing up to forces you to have x numbers and special characters or makes you change your password every y weeks and you're in a position to suggest that this is *not* done then it may be worthwhile doing making that suggestion. The NCSC has talked about this [here](https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/collection/passwords/updating-your-approach#tip5-password-collection).
