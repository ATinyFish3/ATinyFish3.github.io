# Guilty Giving; Good?

## Why Feel Guilty in the First Place?

I think the obvious, intuitive answer to this question is that people feel guilty that they are comparatively better off than those in poorer places or less fortunate circumstances. I, personally, do not think this *is* a good reason to feel guilty; no one has done something that has lead to this difference in circumstances - it's just dumb (un)luck. Furthermore, what is the guilt actually for? If you feel guilt that you are better off - don't feel happy that you get to live a happy life, unprecedented in human history (for the most part). If you feel guilt for people in worse circumstances - why? You had no hand in this - of course there are plenty of justified negative emotions that can be felt towards this disparity, I just don't think that guilt is one of them.

## Guilt is not a Moral Framework

Imagine your moral framework is guilt and you are presented with any kind of trolley-problem-esque decision - what are you to do if you want to avoid guilt? It seems plausible to me that any given person could feel guilty about, for instance, letting 1 person die or letting 5 people die - regardless of whether what you think and what your intuitions would tell you the "better" choice would be. I do not think anyone uses guilt as a moral framework - so why apply it to being altruistic - something that is (or at least can be) a moral decision? The question should simply be whether you believe that saving and improving lives is a morally good thing to do (in which case do it!) not whether you would feel guilty but not saving and improving lives. In fact feeling guilt for not improving lives is probably an indication that your morals point to saving and improving lives as a good thing - but it's that fact, not the mitigation of guilt, that is the important thing.

## Why Not Feeling Guilt is a Good Thing

Not feeling guilty about those less fortunate than you can be beneficial - if you want to do something about them being less fortunate. Imagine that your motivation for  wanting to help people is guilt - surely the act of donating money to charity, as a way to help, would be in itself enough to relieve this guilt. Now imagine your motivation is because you think suffering is bad or that your morals compel you to do so - you may be more motivated to do the most good you can as the reason for donating is to act in the most moral way, not just to do something that is "good enough" to get rid of feelings of guilt.

## Cause Prioritisation Guilt

Maybe, if you are already an effective altruist, you feel guilt at not being able to help with everything; if all your donations got to the Against Malaria Foundation then should you feel guilty that you haven't helped children with vitamin A deficiency by donating to Helen Keller International, or that you haven't helped longtermist causes or animal welfare causes? Maybe this is "valid" however if you are already donating to the most effective causes and prioritising based on what you believe to be the best causes then the only way to improve and help as many causes as possible would be to have an ever increasing amount of resources to do so - an infeasible task that means you should probably not feel guilty about doing the best you are able to do with what you have.
